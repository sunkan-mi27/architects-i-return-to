Personal Study - 4 Architects

Architects I Return To

A running collections of the architects whose ideas keep shaping how i think about space - material, light and care in construction

Study No. 01 - Switzerland, b. 1943

Peter Zumthor

'I've said goodbye to the overworked notion that architecture has to save the world.' - Peter Zumthor

Zumthor builds slowly and rarely, starting each project with a material rather than a form. His small practice in the Swiss village of Haldenstein has produced some of the quietest, most physically intense spaces in contemporary architecture - buildings less concerned with how they look than with how they feel against skin.

  • 1996Thermal BathsVals, Switzerland
  • 1997Kunsthaus BregenzAustria
  • 2011Serpentine PavillionLondon, UK

Study No. 02 - Iraq / UK, 1950-2016

Zaha Hadid

"There are 360 degrees, so why stick to one?" - Zaha Hadid

Hadid treated buildings like terrain instead of boxes, trading right angles for sweeping, continuous curves drawn from landscape and motion. In 2004 she became the first woman to win the pritzker price, and her practice went on to push computer-aided design futher than almost anyone before her.

  • 2009MAXXI MuseumRome, Italy
  • 2010Guangzhou Opera HouseChina
  • 2011London Aquatics CentreLondon, UK

Study No. 03 - Nigeria, 1935

Demas Nwoko

"Whatever you build, it should suit your culture." - Demas Nwoko

Nwoko trained as a painter and sculptor before turning to architecture, and it shows - his buildings read like assembled artworks, full of carved motifs, terracotta brick and deliberate light and shadow.He calls his approach "organic modernism" : merging local building knowledge with modern engineering rather than importing a foreign model wholesale.

  • 1967New Culture StudiosIbadan, Nigeria
  • 1975Dominican InstituteIbadan, Nigeria
  • -Akenzua CulturalBenin City, Nigeria

Study No. 4 - Nigeria, 1929-2000

Oluwole Olumuyiwa

First Nigerian Architecture Graduate to return home and open a practice - Lagos, 1960

Olumuyiwa studied architecture and planning at the University of Machester, then trained across European firms before becoming, in 1958, the first Nigeria architecture graduate to come home. As the first president of the Arcitects Registration Council of Nigeria, he helped define the profession locally while quietly designing many of the country's early modern schools and public buildings.

  • 1960sManagement HouseIdowu Taylor St, Lagos
  • 1960sPublic schools, western NigeriaVarious
